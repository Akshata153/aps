1.Real-Time Product Search (Amazon)

Existing algorithm:

Elasticsearch with TF-IDF.
Elasticsearch with TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is a widely used search engine technology that indexes and retrieves documents based on relevance scores calculated using TF-IDF.
Limitations:
TF-IDF computes document similarity directly in the word-count space, which can be slow for large vocabularies. TF-IDF does not consider the semantic meaning of words: it cannot understand the context of the words and derive their meaning. Moreover, it fails to define semantic links between words.

Proposed algorithm:

Trie-based Search.
Trie-based search is a data structure that efficiently stores and retrieves strings. It is particularly useful for searching for substrings within a large corpus of text.

    Trie-based search algorithms can potentially address some of the limitations of TF-IDF used in Elasticsearch.

    1. Speed and Efficiency:
      • TF-IDF Limitation: TF-IDF calculates document similarity based on word frequencies and inverse document frequencies, which can be computationally intensive for large vocabularies and documents.
      • Trie-based Solution: Trie structures are efficient for prefix matching, allowing for faster retrieval of terms compared to traditional inverted indices used in TF-IDF. This efficiency can speed up search operations, especially in scenarios with large datasets.
    2. Semantic Understanding and Context:
      • TF-IDF Limitation: TF-IDF operates primarily on statistical measures (word frequencies and document frequencies) without directly capturing semantic relationships or understanding the contextual meaning of words.
      • Trie-based Solution: While tries themselves do not inherently add semantic understanding, they can facilitate more nuanced search functionalities. By structuring data hierarchically based on prefixes, tries can potentially be extended or combined with semantic analysis techniques.
    3. Semantic Links Between Words:
      • TF-IDF Limitation: TF-IDF does not establish semantic links between words, which limits its ability to handle synonyms, related terms, or contextually similar terms effectively.
      • Trie-based Solution: Tries can be designed to incorporate relationships between terms or augmentations that capture semantic links. For instance, by linking nodes in a trie with related terms or by integrating semantic embeddings into the trie structure, it's possible to enhance search results by considering synonyms or related concepts.

    Space and Time Complexity Analysis:

    Trie:

    Time complexity:
  • Insertion: O(m) where m is the length of the word
  • Search: O(m) where m is the length of the word.
    Space complexity:
  • O(n * m) in the worst case, where n is the number of words and m is the average length of the words

Elasticsearch with TF-IDF:

    Time complexity:
  • Indexing: O(n) where n is the number of documents.
  • Search: O(q + d) where q is the number of terms in the query and d is the number of matching documents.
    Space complexity:
  • Proportional to the number of unique terms in the indexed documents, optimized through compression and indexing techniques

Why use trie?

To fully leverage the benefits of trie-based search and address the semantic limitations of TF-IDF, integration with semantic technologies or enhanced data structures (like graph databases for semantic relationships) would be necessary. This hybrid approach could combine the efficiency of trie-based indexing with the semantic richness required for understanding context and meaning in text-based searches.
While trie-based search algorithms offer advantages such as efficient substring searches and faster prefix matching, they also come with certain limitations:
Memory Usage: Tries can consume a significant amount of memory, especially for large datasets. Each node in the trie represents a character, and for datasets with a vast vocabulary or long strings, the memory overhead can be substantial.
Time Complexity: Although tries excel in prefix matching and substring searches, their time complexity can be higher compared to other data structures like hash tables or binary search trees, especially when dealing with very large datasets.

Conclusion:

While trie-based search is powerful for specific substring and prefix matching tasks, Elasticsearch with TF-IDF remains a preferred choice for many applications due to its overall efficiency, scalability, and feature-rich capabilities in handling large-scale text search and retrieval operation

2. User Engagement Analysis

User engagement analysis is a crucial aspect of e-commerce platforms as it helps businesses understand how users interact with their products and services. By analyzing user engagement, businesses can identify areas for improvement, optimize their offerings, and improve customer satisfaction.

Algorithm: Kadanes algorithm

Market benefits:

  • It offers significant benefits by identifying peak engagement periods, optimizing content scheduling, and enhancing user interaction strategies, which ultimately improve overall user engagement and satisfaction.

Implementation consideration:

Kadane’s algorithm is employed by treating user engagement metrics (such as clicks, views, or purchases) as an array of values over time. It efficiently computes the maximum subarray sum, pinpointing periods of high activity and facilitating strategic decisions on product promotions, content posting, and user interaction timing.

Challenges:

  • Analyzing user engagement in e-commerce using Kadane’s algorithm poses challenges in accurately distinguishing engagement spikes from noise in the data and adapting the algorithm to handle real-time streaming data.
  • While effective for identifying contiguous segments of maximum engagement, Kadane’s algorithm may struggle with diverse engagement patterns or when dealing with non-contiguous peaks. Scaling Kadane’s for real-time applications with continuously updated data streams requires robust adaptation and integration with other algorithms for comprehensive user engagement analysis.

Time Complexity

3. Dynamic Pricing Adjustments

Dynamic pricing often involves range queries that help determine the optimal price based on various factors such as demand, competitor pricing, and historical sales data.

Algorithm: Segment tree with lazy propogation

Market benefits:

  • Ensures optimal pricing during peak demand periods
  • Efficient Bulk Updates: During a demand surge, prices may need to be adjusted across multiple products simultaneously. Lazy propagation allows for these bulk updates to be deferred and applied only when necessary, reducing the overhead of immediate updates
  • Reduced Update Time: Without lazy propagation, each update to the segment tree would require traversing and updating all relevant nodes, leading to a high time complexity, especially for large datasets. Lazy propagation ensures updates are marked and propagated in O(logn) time, significantly improving performance
  • Optimal Resource Utilization: In peak demand periods, system resources can be strained. Lazy propagation helps in spreading the computational load, applying updates only when queries are made, thus optimizing the use of CPU and memory resources.
  • Consistency and Accuracy: Ensures that all deferred updates are accurately applied before any query results are returned. This consistency is crucial for dynamic pricing, where real-time accuracy directly impacts revenue and customer satisfaction
  • Scalability: Allows the system to handle a large number of price adjustments efficiently, making it scalable for high-traffic periods. This is essential for e-commerce platforms that experience significant fluctuations in user activity.

Time complexity:

  • Build:O(n)
  • Update:O(logn)

Space complexity:

  • O(n)

Limitations:

  • Complex Implementation
  • Although segment trees with lazy propagation are space-efficient for range queries and updates, they still consume more memory .
  • While deferred updates improve performance, they introduce a delay in the immediate application of updates.

5. Stock availability check

The stock availability check ensures that products are within the desired price range and in stock. This functionality uses a segment tree to efficiently manage and query stock status and price information, enabling e-commerce platforms to maintain optimal inventory levels and prevent stockouts.

Algorithm: Range Minimum/Maximum Query (RMQ)

Implementation:

A segment tree is constructed where each node represents a range of products, and the value at each node holds the minimum or maximum stock count (or price) for that range. To check if products are within a desired price range or if a specific product is in stock, the segment tree is queried for the minimum or maximum values within the relevant range.When stock levels change due to sales or restocking, the segment tree is updated to reflect the new stock counts.

Market analysis:

  • Prevents Stockouts: By maintaining accurate and real-time stock information, e-commerce platforms can prevent stockouts, ensuring that popular products are always available for purchase.
  • Improves Customer Satisfaction: Quick and reliable stock availability checks lead to a better shopping experience, as customers are less likely to encounter out-of-stock items or unexpected price changes
  • Optimizes Inventory Management: Efficient stock management helps businesses reduce excess inventory and associated costs, while ensuring that products are available when needed.

Time complexity:

  • Build:O(n) ,where n is number of products.
  • Query:O(logn) for min/max queries
  • Update: O(logn) for updating stock counts or prices.

Space complexity:

  • O(n), as the segment tree stores aggregated data for each range of products

Comparision with existing are:

Some platforms rely on SQL queries to retrieve stock information. While more efficient than linear scans, complex queries can still be slow and resource-intensive, particularly with large datasets.
Magento (Older Versions): Some older versions of Magento may rely on basic SQL queries for stock checks.
Drawbacks: Complex queries can still be slow and resource-intensive, particularly with large datasets.

Advantage of using Segment trees with min/max query

  • Efficient Queries: Segment trees allow for O(logn) time complexity for range minimum/maximum queries, making them significantly faster than linear scans or complex SQL queries.
  • Real-Time Updates: Segment trees can be updated in O(logn) time, ensuring that stock information remains current without significant performance overhead.
  • Scalability: As inventories grow, segment trees maintain efficient performance, unlike linear scans and complex SQL queries, which can become increasingly slow and resource-intensive.

Limitations

  • Implementing and maintaining a segment tree requires a good understanding of data structures and algorithms. Developers need to handle edge cases such as tree balancing, which can add complexity to the codebase.
  • Segment trees can consume more memory compared to simpler data structures, especially for large datasets. Each node in the tree stores additional metadata, and maintaining balance may require additional overhead.
  • Setting up and initializing a segment tree requires additional development time and effort compared to using built-in database functionalities or simpler data structures.

14.Accurate Delivery Charge Calculation

Delivery charge calculation is usually not shown to users.Hence to provide transparent and accurate delivery charges to its users. The optimal route and distance between the user's location and the selected warehouse or store must be efficiently calculated.This is crucial for ensuring fair pricing for customers and delivery partners, building trust, and maintaining a positive user experience.

Algorithm: Dijkstra's algorithm

Dijkstra's algorithm is a graph traversal algorithm that finds the shortest path between two nodes in a graph. It is used to calculate the optimal route and distance between the user's location and the selected warehouse or store.


Market benefits:

  • Providing accurate and transparent delivery charges builds customer trust and satisfaction.
  • Fair pricing attracts and retains customers, leading to increased order volume and revenue.
  • Fair compensation for delivery partners based on accurate distance calculations improves their satisfaction and motivation.

Implementation consideration:

Represent the road network as a weighted graph, where nodes represent intersections or points of interest, and edges represent road segments with associated weights.
The algorithm will explore the graph, keeping track of the shortest distance to each node, and eventually return the shortest path and distance.
Calculate the fare based on the distance obtained from Dijkstra’s algorithm, considering additional factors such as delivery charges per kilometer, traffic conditions, and peak hours.
Base fare is a fixed charge. Fare per kilometer is multiplied by the shortest path distance to determine the total delivery charge.

Challenges:

  • Dijkstra's algorithm may struggle with large, complex road networks with numerous intersections and road segments, leading to longer computation times and potential errors.
  • Calculating the distance between two points on a map while considering real-world factors such as traffic conditions, road closures, and one-way streets.
  • Accessing and maintaining reliable map data that reflects current road conditions.

Time Complexity

  • It is typically O(V2) when using a simple array implementation or O((V + E) log V) with a priority queue, where V represents the number of vertices and E represents the number of edges

Space Complexity

  • It is typically O(V) to O(E + V), where V is the number of vertices and E is the number of edges in the graph
